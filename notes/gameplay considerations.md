    ** Card Advantage:
This a concept that comes from MTG strategy, and is effectively just "How many cards do I have and how does that compare to my opponents?"
In the case of a card-shedding game like Mao, instead we will use this concept to consider "How many cards can enter or leave my hand in one turn?"

In a given turn, absent of any rules but the Basics, you can draw a card (+1 adv) and play a card (-1 adv).
Because the aim of the game is to shed cards, the lower your adv, the better.

the ideal turn is to play a card without having to draw (-1 adv for the turn).
A slightly less ideal turn is having to draw a card, but the drawn card is playable (+0 adv).
The worst case scenario for a turn is having to draw a card and you still not having any playables (+1 adv).

This is important for players to consider when dealing with both their own hand and their opponents - any player with one card left might be able to shed it on their turn (this is why a "Mao"/"UNO"/whatever call when passing with only one card left is a compulsory action - it stops players from winning 'out of nowhere'), and changes how other players play - they might choose to change the top card away from something that would benefit them more in order to reduce the number of 'outs' (range of cards that the player in Mao must have to win).

> THIS IS WHY ONE OF THE MOST POWERFUL EFFECTS IN THIS GAME IS SHEDDING MORE THAN ONE CARD A TURN (-2 adv) - IT LETS PLAYERS WIN "OUT OF NOWHERE"
WHICH SUCKS BECAUSE PLAYING "PAIRS" (two cards of the same rank at the same time) THE FIRST EFFECT THAT EVERYONE THINKS OF 
> any rule that allows for the shedding of more than one card a turn must carry some level of risk or be a rare effect or something else - IT CANNOT BE FREE

> The ability to go +0 at will for a turn is also quite powerful - this is what the Cycling mechanic is intended to provide

> The ability to change the top of the deck is also powerful, because it can deny an opponent who has to draw a useful card from them
^ this forces them to perhaps go +1 for their turn, unless they can also do something.

    ** Rule Rarity

Some effects are incredibly powerful or disruptive - to moderate their impact on the game we can 'lock' them behind specific rarities:
"When you play a [...]" triggers:

[COMMON]: 5-13
[UNCOMMON]: 3-4
[RARE]: 2
[SINGULAR]: 1


    ** Annoying Rules:
This *can* include rules that are specifically designed to piss you off, but is mostly considering how much of a hassle the actions involved are.
For example: Blackjack is an 'annoying' rule - everyone has to put down their cards, and 10-20 new cards need to be dealt out, tracking who wins and who loses, before we all pick up our cards again and continue playing

> These rules must not present themselves often - ragebait rules especially - players are not likely to stay with the game
